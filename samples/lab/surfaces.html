Parametrization examples

These examples show parametrically rendered two-dimensional surfaces.

A surface is described parametrically with a function f ( u , v ) = ( x , y , z ) T from parameters (u, v) to a three-dimensional position vector. The domains of u and v vary per parametrization.

In the examples below, parameter n is the number of samples in each of the two directions. The computation complexity is therefore n squared.

Parameter m is the density of the normal and other vectors. If m equals 1 then arrows are shown for every line segment. If m equals 2 then are shown every other segment, and so on. Reducing the number of arrows helps performance.

Parameter d is a scaling factor for the arrows. Increasing d increases the arrow sizes.

Plane

Parametrization of a plane.

f ( u , v ) = ( x , y , z ) T with

  • x = u
  • y = v
  • z = a + b x + c y
where a is an offset, b and c are scaling factors, -8 ≤ u ≤ 8 , and -8 ≤ v ≤ 8

Paraboloid

Plot of a paraboloid surface by revolutions.

f ( u , v ) = ( x , y , z ) T with

  • x = u cos (v)
  • y = u sin (v)
  • z = a u 2
where a is a scaling factor, -5 ≤ u ≤ 5 , and - 1 2 π ≤ v ≤ 1 2 π

The square u 2 for z makes its unit metre squared.

Saddle

Plot of a saddle surface.

f ( u , v ) = ( x , y , z ) T with

  • x = u
  • y = v
  • z = a u 2 + b v 2
where a and b are a scaling factors, -5 ≤ u ≤ 5 , and -5 ≤ v ≤ 5

The squares u 2 and v 2 for z makes its unit metre squared.

Setting a and b to the parameter of the paraboloid gives the same surface, but parameterized differently.

Ellipsoid

Parametrization of an ellipsoid surface.

f ( u , v ) = ( x , y , z ) T with

  • x = a cos (u) sin (v)
  • y = b sin (u) sin (v)
  • z = c cos (v)
where a , b , and c are scaling factors in the three dimensions, 0 ≤ u ≤ 2π , and 0 ≤ v ≤ π

Torus

Parametrization of a torus surface.

f ( u , v ) = ( x , y , z ) T with

  • x = ( a + b cos ( u )) cos ( v )
  • y = ( a + b cos ( u )) sin ( v )
  • z = b sin ( u )
where a is the major radius, b the minor radius, 0 ≤ u ≤ 2π , and 0 ≤ v ≤ 2π

Möbius

The Möbius strip.

f ( u , v ) = ( x , y , z ) T with

  • x = a cos ( u ) ( c + v cos ( 1 2 u ))
  • x = a sin ( u ) ( c + v cos ( 1 2 u ))
  • z = a v sin ( 1 2 u )
where a and c are scaling factors, -π ≤ u ≤ π , and -0.5 ≤ v ≤ 0.5

The surface is non-orientable. A consequence is that no smooth varying normals exist. In the image a discontinuity can be seen where the normals 'jump' to the other side.

The blue and purple paths are in a diagonal direction, yet they terminate at the starting point.

The orange line along the surface also terminates a its starting point, showing there is just one edge. The orange edge path has double length, therefore it has normals on both sides.
